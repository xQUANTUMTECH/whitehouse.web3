Welcome to White House 3.0

A provocative economic manifesto against protectionism and stagnation. Milton Friedman taught us that economic freedom is the foundation of all progress: no tariffs, no walls, just competition.

Today, his ideas are more alive than ever – and those who understand them, like certain visionaries with rockets and electric cars, deserve applause. While Elon Musk faces criticism from the likes of Navarro, the real issue is clear: free trade benefits everyone.

Friedman Was Right

Free Markets

Friedman's relentless logic: tariffs don't protect, they strangle. His 'Open Economy' is the recipe for a world that innovates.

Minimal Government

Low taxes, minimal intervention – allowing innovation to flourish without bureaucratic barriers.

Upward Mobility

Economic freedom creates opportunities for all, regardless of background or circumstances.

"The great virtue of a free market system is that it does not care what color people are; it does not care what their religion is; it only cares whether they can produce something you want to buy." — Milton Friedman

Tariffs: The Past That Doesn't Learn

Tariffs are taxes disguised as patriotism. Friedman said it clearly: 'There is nothing more inefficient than protecting those who cannot compete.'

Someone out there – yes, that Tesla guy – is shouting on X: zero tariffs between the US and Europe. It's not sycophancy, it's mathematics. Friedman would approve: the global market is the only one that works.

While Peter Navarro calls Musk a "moron," history will show who truly understands economics.

Less State, More Freedom

Modern champions of Friedman's vision

Javier Milei: The Chainsaw Revolution

While Milton Friedman's ideas continue to inspire economists worldwide, Argentina's Javier Milei has taken up the mantle of free-market radicalism with his bold "chainsaw plan" to slash the state. His economic philosophy echoes Friedman's core principles: less government, more private sector, and unfettered market freedom.

Milei's rallying cry against the "parasitic state" follows Friedman's view that "the government solution to a problem is usually as bad as the problem." His reforms aim to dismantle regulations, privatize state-owned enterprises, and reduce the burden of government – a modern incarnation of Friedman's vision.

"The state is not the solution. The state is the problem itself." — Javier Milei
